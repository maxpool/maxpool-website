⚛️ The Half-Life Framework

Understanding AI Failure Through Survival Analysis

Toby Ord's breakthrough insight: AI agent performance follows the same mathematical patterns as radioactive decay. Each agent has a measurable "half-life" - the task duration where success probability drops to 50%.

P(success) = e^(-λt)
where λ = ln(2) / half_life
and t = task duration

Key Reliability Relationships

Target Success Rate Required Task Length Reduction Practical Implication
80% 1/3 of half-life duration Suitable for monitored automation
90% 1/7 of half-life duration Production-ready with fallbacks
99% 1/70 of half-life duration Autonomous operation possible
99.9% 1/700 of half-life duration Mission-critical applications

📈 Exponential Decay Curves

Success probability decreases exponentially with task duration, following P(t) = e^(-λt)

Why This Matters

The half-life model reveals that current AI lacks error recovery mechanisms. Each minute of operation carries a constant failure probability, compounding over time. This explains why "just make the context longer" doesn't solve reliability problems.

The Reliability Cliff

The exponential nature of both curves creates a "reliability cliff" - tasks slightly beyond current capabilities have near-zero success rates, but become suddenly feasible as capabilities improve. This explains why AI progress often feels discontinuous.

Persistent Challenges

  • Compounding Errors: Small mistakes early in tasks cascade into failures
  • Lack of True Recovery: No mechanism to "checkpoint and rollback" like humans
  • Context Degradation: Performance decreases with context length, even within limits
  • Unstructured Tasks: Open-ended problems without clear success criteria remain difficult
  • Real-World Complexity: Benchmarks may not capture production environment challenges

💡 Practical Implications for AI Engineering

Design Patterns for Current Reliability Levels

The 7-Minute Rule

For 90% reliability with current models (2025), keep individual agent tasks under 7 minutes. Design systems that checkpoint frequently and can restart from known good states.

Recommended Architecture Patterns

Pattern When to Use Reliability Gain
Task Decomposition Always - break tasks into <5 minute chunks 2-3× improvement
Parallel Attempts Critical tasks - run 3 agents, take majority Up to 99.9% from 90%
Human Checkpoints Every 15-30 minutes of agent work Prevents cascade failures
State Verification After each subtask completion Early error detection
Rollback Capability For any state-changing operations Recovery from failures

🎯 Conclusion

The Path to Reliability

AI reliability isn't about sudden breakthroughs - it's about steady exponential improvement in failure rates. By understanding both the half-life model (why AI fails) and growth patterns (how fast it's improving), we can make informed decisions about when and how to deploy AI agents.

The convergence of these two research approaches provides a framework for predicting when specific automation thresholds become feasible. Organizations that understand these dynamics can time their investments optimally - neither too early (wasting resources on unreliable systems) nor too late (missing competitive advantages).

Key Takeaways

  • 🎯 Current State (2025): 50-minute tasks at 50% reliability, 7-minute tasks at 90%
  • 📈 Growth Rate: Doubling every 7 months, highly consistent since 2019
  • 🔮 2030 Projection: Month-long tasks achievable, week-long at 99% reliability
  • ⚙️ Design Principle: Architect systems around current reliability limits, not hoped-for improvements
  • 💡 Investment Strategy: Phase adoption based on task duration and required reliability
